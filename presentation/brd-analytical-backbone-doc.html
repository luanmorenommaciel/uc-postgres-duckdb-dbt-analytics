1 · Executive Summary

We operate a high-volume e-commerce platform — roughly $500M in annual GMV across 50,000 orders per day (75,000 at peak). A single PostgreSQL database serves both the storefront and analytics, and the two workloads now contend for the same resources. The result is slow insight, fragility under peak load, and an engineering team spending most of its time keeping the system alive rather than improving it.

The situation

Operational transactions and analytical queries share one database. As volume has grown, heavy analytical scans lock resources during peak traffic — degrading customer-facing order processing — while the transactional write load makes reporting crawl. This is an architectural mismatch, not a tuning problem.

The ask

Separate the two workloads so analytics no longer contends with the storefront: faster insight, isolation under peak, and a sharp reduction in operational toil. Every requirement in this document is stated as a verifiable outcome tied to a current → target metric. The cost of inaction is approximately $2.08M per year in run-rate plus the opportunity cost of a data team consumed by maintenance.

The one-line case

A core analytical query takes 12 minutes where the business needs it in under 5 seconds — 144× too slow. The fix is structural separation, and it pays back inside a year.

2 · The Problem — One Database, Two Jobs

The same PostgreSQL instance is asked to do two fundamentally different jobs at once. They have opposite needs, and at our volume they actively interfere.

Workload A · Operational
The storefront

Many small, fast writes — orders, payments, inventory. Needs low latency and predictable response, every second, especially at peak.

Workload B · Analytical
Reporting & insight

Few large, heavy reads — aggregations across history. Needs to scan a lot of data without a deadline pressing on each query.

Where they collide

Collision · Peak
Analytics degrades checkout

A heavy report run during peak traffic locks resources the storefront needs — slowing order processing exactly when volume is highest.

Collision · Freshness
Reporting can't keep up

The transactional write load and contention push analytics to a 24-hour batch cadence — decisions are made on yesterday's numbers.

The structural point

You cannot tune your way out of two workloads with opposite needs sharing one engine. The only durable fix is to give analytics its own lane.

The contention problem reaches the business on three fronts.

$2.08M annual run-rate, ~$696K of it avoidable toil. Plus the unmeasured cost of decisions delayed by 24-hour-stale data.

Operational

6 · Requirements & Acceptance Criteria

Each requirement is a verifiable outcome — a future test could pass or fail it — and each ties to a business KPI as current → target. No technology is prescribed; the solution chooses how.

R1 · PEAK ISOLATION
Analytics must not degrade the storefront.
Verifiable: under peak load (75,000 orders/day), analytical queries cause zero measurable degradation to transactional latency.
R2 · QUERY LATENCY
Analytical queries return fast enough to act on.
KPI: p95 latency 12 min → < 5 sec; p99 28 min → < 15 sec.
R3 · FRESHNESS
Reporting reflects recent reality, not yesterday.
KPI: event-to-reportable lag 24 hr → near-real-time (minutes, not hours).
R4 · SELF-SERVE ACCESS
Business users can ask questions without engineering.
Verifiable: a defined set of analytical questions answerable directly by non-engineers, without a new report build each time.
R5 · OPERATIONAL TOIL
The team builds more than it firefights.
KPI: maintenance/incident share 60–70% → < 30% of capacity; incidents 2.8/mo → < 0.5/mo; MTTR 5.2 hr → 30 min.
R6 · AVAILABILITY
The analytical service is dependable.
KPI: availability 97.8% → 99.5%.

7 · Scope — In and Out

In scope

  • Separating analytical workloads from the transactional database.
  • A dependable, fast analytical store the business can query.
  • Keeping analytical data current to within minutes of the source.
  • Self-serve access to a defined set of analytical questions.
  • Reducing the team's maintenance and incident burden.

Out of scope (this phase)

  • Personalization / recommendation engines.
  • Real-time machine-learning use cases.
  • Replacing the storefront or its transactional database.
  • Changes to the order, payment, or fulfillment business logic.
  • Net-new data sources beyond the current operational system.
Why the line sits here

This phase proves the separation and reclaims the operational headroom. Personalization and real-time ML are real opportunities, but they depend on this foundation and are deferred to a later phase so this one stays verifiable.

9 · Constraints & Assumptions

Constraint
No storefront disruption

The solution must be built and cut over without degrading the live storefront or its data.

Constraint
Same team

Delivered with the current data team of eight; no headcount increase assumed in the business case.

Assumption
Source of truth unchanged

The transactional database remains the system of record; the analytical store is derived from it, not a replacement.

Assumption
Stable demand baseline

Volume (50K/day, 75K peak) and the current cost structure are representative for the planning horizon.
